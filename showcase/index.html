The detail that matters

The EUR52 million that wasn't

52,243,584

That figure sat in the November revenue column of the export. The unit's normal month is around EUR5 million, and its costs and payroll did not move. A rushed close builds a growth story around a number like that, and it looks spectacular right up until someone checks. This system flagged it as a probable data entry error, kept it out of every calculation and every sentence of commentary, and asked for a correction at source. The operations data backed that call: 18 projects delivered that month, against 17 planned. A typo stayed a typo, and the report says so.

For each team

One page per business unit, ready to hand out

Group reporting is half the job. The other half is giving each business unit a view it can act on, and that is usually what falls off the table when the close runs late.

Each page shows where the year stands against budget, what drove payroll (people or pay rates) and revenue (how many projects, at what value), which gaps have a documented cause, which need a follow-up, and what next quarter looks like.

Production's page, for example, splits its payroll gap into a headcount effect (an average 54.6 people against 55 planned) plus a small rate effect, and its revenue gap into 140 projects delivered against 141 planned plus a price effect. That is the level where a conversation with a manager actually starts.

Under the hood, in plain terms

How it works

It tidies the export: dates, unit names, duplicates, amounts typed as text. Every fix is listed in a data quality report; nothing is changed silently.

It measures every line against budget, all 840 of them, and speaks only when a gap is big enough to matter.

It explains a gap only when a dated note from the business corroborates it. Otherwise the report says "no clear driver identified" and recommends a follow-up. It never invents a cause.

It refreshes the next quarter's forecast, keeping one-off events out of the base so last year's accident does not become next year's plan.

It drafts the commentary and assembles the pack, then stops. A human reviews and signs. There is no send button.

Where is the AI in all this? In exactly one place: turning the two finished reports into readable commentary. It never sees the raw data, only the aggregated results, and that boundary is re-verified on every run. Every figure in its prose is traced back to a source figure; if one cannot be traced, the run fails. Everything else is ordinary, inspectable calculation that gives the same answer every time.

Why you could trust it

Built the way finance already works

No invented numbers

Commentary is checked figure by figure against the source reports, with a tight tolerance. Anything untraceable fails the run before a human ever sees the pack.

Honest about what it doesn't know

16 of the 20 material variances had no documented cause. The pack says so and lists them as follow-ups. A gap with no evidence is a task, not a story.

A human signs, every time

The pack is assembled as a draft with a review and sign-off block. The system has no way to send anything anywhere, by design.

Your data stays yours

Only one step talks to an AI service, it only receives aggregated summaries, and a check on every run verifies the raw data never leaves the machine.
